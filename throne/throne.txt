Chapter 1

The echo of ancient fear. 

Why do I feel terror just when I am closest to fulfillment? Throughout the centuries, the human soul has been trained in pain. Since the dawn of civilizations, since the days when man looked up to the sky with superstitious dread, we've been conditioned to associate well-being with danger, joy with threat, rising with inevitable downfall. This dark association etched like fire into the collective memories of entire generations still echoes deep within each of us. An ancient echo. But it's important to understand it is not my true self who fears fulfillment. The spirit of God within me longs for the full expression of life, of health, of love, of abundance. The inner spirit knows no fear, has no hesitation in growing, in prospering, in reigning. But the carnal mind, that old program based on separation from God, does fear. It fears because it does not understand the true nature of life. It sees light, and in its confusion, perceives only threat. It senses happiness and in its distortion braces for catastrophe. When a person after much spiritual and mental effort begins to approach the manifestation of their desires, be it material achievement, inner realization, loving relationship or a life of service. Something deep within my unconscious may awaken a reflex of alarm. The old voice whispers, "This can't last. You don't deserve this much good. Something bad is about to happen." And though consciously I long for success and happiness, my voice, if left unchallenged, begins to plant small doubts, subtle tensions, tiny misalignments that, like steady drops of water, slowly erode the foundation of the new life. Why does this happen? It happens because generation after generation, I've been taught to mistrust wholeness. Old sayings reflect it. After laughter comes tears, if something's too good to be true, it probably is. Don't count your chickens before they hatch. These thoughts, repeated like fear-filled mantras, have carved a pattern of preventive self-destruction into human consciousness. If I'm too happy, I'd better brace for the fall. If I've gained something beautiful, I must fear its loss. But this fear is not natural. It does not come from God. It comes from the false belief that life is a battlefield, not a garden of creation. It comes from ignorance of the law of good that upholds the universe. To free myself from the echo of ancient fear, I must first recognize it. I cannot heal what I deny. I must face the programming with(1)in and say, "This fear is not mine. I inherited it from a world that still does not know its unity with God. But I am not of that world. I am of God." The moment I feel fear, just as everything is going well, is paradoxically a sign of growth. It is the evidence that I am crossing an inner threshold, breaking through boundaries that have long felt safe. Fear is the protest of an old consciousness being displaced by light. So I do not be alarmed when fear arises. I do not believe its presence signals failure. Rather see it for what it is. At the whisper of a dying shadow resisting its own end. I remember fear is a lie. A lie so old it seems real. But it is still a lie. I was not made to fall the moment I touch heaven. I was made to dwell in the heaven of God. To move from glory to glory, from blessing to blessing, from life to life. The inner spirit in me never fears when life blossoms. The inner spirit in me says, "I have come that I may have life and have it in abundance." So how do I overcome the echo of fear? First, awareness. I am aware that fear is an old program, not a present reality. Second, declaration. I affirm with my whole soul whenever fear arises. I'm not a slave to fear. I am free in the love of God. My good is legitimate, lasting, and secure. Third, spiritual action, I do not stop. I do not retreat. And I do not negotiate with doubt. I move forward as if fear did not exist because in truth it doesn't. It is only a mist that vanishes the moment I walk toward the light. Wholeness is not an accident. It is not alone. Wholeness is your destiny. The echo of fear may scream, may cry, may beg me to return to the barren land of doubt. But I am not from there. I am from the kingdom of God. And in that kingdom, happiness is the rule, not the exception. As I continue through this book, I will break one by one the invisible chains that bind me to old fear. I will learn to recognize, confront, and heal. Not to achieve success as the world defines it, but to live in true success. The conscious, joyful, permanent union with the good that God has already established for me since before the foundation of the world. The echo of ancient fear only holds power if I lend it my ear. Today is the day I can choose to listen to another voice, the voice of truth which says, "Do not be afraid. I am with you. Where I am now is where I've always belonged in my heart." And from there, no one can snatch me. No one can bring me down. No one can separate me from my own wholeness. So be it. 

Chapter 2 

I am not my thought. Separating truth from mental illusion. 

One of the greatest sources of suffering for the human soul is the confusion between the true self and the fleeting thoughts that pass through the mind. Since ancient times, humans have believed that the thoughts that bombard them, good or bad, bright or shadowed, are part of their identity. Nothing could be further from the truth. I am not your thought. I am the one who observes the thought. I am the eternal creation of God made in God's image and likeness, invulnerable in my essence. To understand this is not a mere philosophical exercise. It is a matter of spiritual life or death. Because as long as I continue to believe that I am those thoughts of fear, doubt, unworthiness, or self-deeat, I will remain trapped in a mental labyrinth without knowing the way out. Human thought, as I experience it today, is mostly automatic, chaotic, and a reflection of old collective suggestions. Rarely does an undisiplined mind produce original or spiritual thoughts. The natural mind is like a forgotten record player repeating the same programs of fear, lack, danger, and insufficiency. That is why it is vital to separate what I think from who I am. When a thought of fear, harsh self-criticism, pessimism, or hopelessness arises in my mind, I do not say, "I am lost." Instead, I say, "This is a mistaken thought. It is not mine. It has no authority over me. It does not come from God." Here is the truth. If a thought does not reflect love, peace, power, wisdom, faith, joy, or life, then it does not come from God. And if it doesn't come from God, it has no right to rule my consciousness. Just as a traveler can watch gray clouds pass without being affected in their being. I must learn to watch negative thoughts pass without attaching myself to them. I do not bless them with my agreement. I do not give them citizenship in my soul. Look at them the way you'd watch dry leaves blown by the wind. Fleeting, hollow, harmless, unless I cling to them. The great error is to identify with every thought that crosses the mind. To say, "I'm a failure," just because a thought of failure passed through my head, is like saying, "I'm a beggar," just because I saw one on the street. I am not what I see. I am not what you think on the surface level of the mind. I am what God thought when he created me. And what did God think? He thought me glorious, invincible, eternal, pure and full of light. Now what is mental illusion? Mental illusion is any belief rooted in separation from God. Any thought that tells me that I am alone, forsaken, weak, or unworthy is an illusion. It may feel very real emotionally, but its reality is like a ghost in the night that vanishes the moment I turn on the light. So how do I separate truth from illusion? First, pause. I don't react immediately to the thought that arises. I observe it. I ask myself calmly, does this thought lead me closer to love, peace, and life, or does it drag me toward guilt, fear, and despair? Second, compare. I search my spiritual memory for the eternal words. I know, God says, "I am with you."  I learn, "I can do all things through God who strengthens me." I sing, "God is my guidance. I shall not wander." Any thought that contradicts these eternal truths is illusion, even if it comes dressed in logic or common sense. Third, I replace. It's not enough to reject the false thought. I must actively replace it with a true one. So when the mind says I am going to lose everything, I respond within myself, God's good is permanent in my life. Nothing true can be lost. This is the sacred work of every spiritual traveler to discern between the voice of fear and the voice of the spirit. I do not fight negative thoughts. I do not try to repress them by force. I simply look at them as clouds drifting across a sky that remains blue. No matter the storm, my soul, my true self, my inner spirit does not change because dark thoughts pass by. My essence remains radiant, powerful and perfect. With every false thought that dissolves, my true nature shines a little brighter. I won't be afraid of the thoughts that cross my mind. I remember, the ocean is not changed in its essence by the winds that stir its surface. In its depths, the sea remains calm, vast, and majestic. And so does my deeper self, untouched by the fleeting winds of surface thought. Every time I remember, I am not my thought, I reclaim my true self. Every time I say, "This is just a passing thought, but I am eternal." I strengthen my divine identity. Because in the end it's not about having perfect thoughts all the time. It's about always remembering who I am, even in the midst of imperfect ones. And who I am, no one can take from me. I am a child of God. I am made in the image of eternal life. I am free now and forever. And that is the truth that will set me free. 

Chapter 3 

The invisible sabotage. How the conditioned mind operates to deny my success. 

In the silent depth of every human soul, there lies an unshakable yearning for expansion, for growth, for fulfillment. That sacred flame which cannot be extinguished is the very life of God flowing within me. Always urging me to be more, to express more, to live in abundance. And yet I cannot ignore the painful observation that time and time again when good begins to approach my life, an inner force seems to conspire to reject it, minimize it, or destroy it. This force is the invisible saboteur, the conditioned mind. The conditioned mind is not evil in itself. It is not a demon or a personal curse. It is simply a mental structure that through years of repetition (2) has come to act like an automatic program. Its only purpose paradoxically is to preserve the security of the status quo. And for this old mind, success, expansion, and full happiness represent risks, not blessings. Risks because they imply change, movement, exposure, responsibility. Risks because they challenge deep beliefs, old images I have held about who I am and how the world works. The conditioned mind operates like a fearful watchman. It observes every attempt of me to fly higher, to love more fully, to create with greater courage, and it triggers its internal alarms. This is dangerous. Then what if I fail? What if I lose everything you've gained? What if I am betrayed? Who am I to believe I can reach such heights? These thoughts which seem personal and spontaneous are actually automatic reflections of a belief system installed from childhood, reinforced by past experiences and by the mental atmosphere of a society that fears true success more than it fears the resignation of failure. The invisible sabotage is not always loud. Many times it is subtle, disguised as prudence, realism, or maturity. It shows up as that little voice advising me not to shine too brightly, not to trust too much in my own strength, not to love too intensely, not to celebrate my joy too much. It is the whisper that says it better prepare for the worst. This saboteur does not need to destroy me in one fell swoop. Its favorite method is slow erosion, a doubtfilled thought here, a delayed decision there, a gesture of self-criticism disguised as modesty, a silent surrender of a true desire. Gradually, the soul begins to retract, to reduce its flight, to shrink itself into a smaller, more acceptable, safer mold. Now, why does my mind do this? Because for the conditioned mind, the known, no matter how limiting or painful, is safer than the unknown. The old mind prefers a known suffering to the happiness that comes from stepping into the unknown. Thus, at the very moment my soul is ready to take a big leap into a fuller life, the conditioned mind activates all its defense mechanisms, fear, doubt, guilt, confusion, distraction. At this point, if I am not conscious, I might sabotage my own success in many varied ways. Starting projects with enthusiasm only to abandon them halfway. Accepting opportunities, but then delaying their execution, attracting beautiful relationships, but sabotaging them with distrust or fear, making financial progress, but generating unnecessary expenses or poor decisions that consume my advances. None of this happens because I am incapable or unworthy or because goodness is not meant for me. It happens because the conditioned mind, if it is not observed and corrected, will fight to keep me within its zone of familiarity. The good news is this. I am not the conditioned mind. I am the one who can observe it, correct it, and transcend it. How do I stop the invisible sabotage? First, I must cultivate a loving vigilance over my own thoughts and attitudes. Not with harshness, not with judgment, but with the clear intention to recognize when I am acting from fear and not from faith. Second, it is crucial to actively affirm my right to good things. Every time I feel the temptation to sabotage myself, I stop and decree, "It is safe for me to succeed. It is safe for me to be happy. It is safe for me to live in abundance." This simple but powerful decree begins to re-educate my mind. It teaches my mind that success is not dangerous, that happiness is not betrayal, that expansion is not a threat but the legitimate expression of who I am in God. Third, I learn to move forward in spite of the fear. I do not wait until I feel no fear to act. I fear, while the old mind exists, may continue to whisper, but I do not have to obey it. True spiritual courage does not lie in the absence of fear, but in the refusal to give into it. Every time I act in favor of your expansion despite the echo of fear, my soul strengthens, my mind is re-educated, and the invisible sabotage loses its power over me. I always remember, true success is not an anomaly to be feared. True success is the outward manifestation of a consciousness that has remembered its union with good. I am not betraying anyone when I prosper. I am not being arrogant when I accept your blessings. I am not sinning against God when I live with joy, freedom, and power. On the contrary, I am honoring the life God gave me. I am saying with my existence, "Thy will be done," which is good, perfect, and pleasing. The invisible saboteur only reigns as long as I do not see it. Once exposed to the light of my awareness, it begins to dissolve like fog before the sun. And then the way is cleared. Then I can fly, not to escape my previous life, but to fully embody the glorious destiny that has been mine from the beginning of time. Step by step, thought by thought, action by action, I will conquer the territory of my own soul until sabotage is only a distant memory and success, a natural expression of my true being. This is my destiny, and nothing except my own thoughts can stop me from reaching it. Now I know, now I can choose, and now true freedom begins. 
   
Chapter 4

False humility. When I confuse love for God with rejection of myself.

There is an enemy of the soul even more subtle than fear. False humility. Many who desire to serve God, many who long to live in goodness, unknowingly fall into the snare of this spiritual trap. False humility is a distortion of the truth. It is a perversion of virtue. It is an unconscious attempt most of the time to please God by lowering oneself to unworthiness. Nothing pleases God less than this. In the early stages of spiritual awakening, it is natural for the soul recognizing the infinite greatness of the creator to feel overwhelmed by its own apparent smallness. The man who until yesterday walked blind begins to glimpse the vastness of life, the majesty of the divine law, and he feels like a grain of sand in an ocean without shores. This initial feeling of smallness may contain purity, a true form of humility. But if it is not corrected, it can degenerate into self-loathing, into the denial of one's spiritual dignity, into a permanent sabotage of one's own good. True humility is recognizing that I am not the source, but I am the child of the source. True humility is saying of myself, I can do nothing, but the father who dwells in me, he does the works. False humility, on the other hand, says, I am nothing. I deserve nothing. I can do nothing. And this more than virtue is blasphemy because it denies the image of God in me. It is spitting upon the work of the creator. It is considering that the divine spark within me is defective, insufficient, unworthy. What a grave mistake. What a painful affront to the truth. The love for God is never manifested in self-hatred. The love for God is manifested in recognizing his work within me, in loving what God has created, in honoring the life God has placed in my hands. When I confuse humility with self-denial, I fall into a spiral of fear, guilt, and resignation. And then success seems suspicious. Joy seems inappropriate. Abundance seems sinful. Expansion seems prideful. Thus, without realizing it, I sabotage myself every time a legitimate good comes into my life. I reject it in the name of a holiness that God never asked of me. I condemn myself to mediocrity, in the name of a non-sacrifice that only stems from ignorance. God is not glorified in my misery. God does not take pleasure in my defeat. God does not grow by my shrinking. God is manifested in my fullness. And God is revealed in my true joy. God expresses  in my constant growth. The inner spirit says, "I have come that I might have life and that I might have it more abundantly." Does it mean, "I have come that I might live in scarcity, in fear, in resignation." Do I learn to flagagillate myself, to despise myself, to sabotage my blessings? No. I learn true humility, recognizing that all good comes from God, but also recognizing that God dwells within me and that God's will is for you to live, (3) grow, and bear fruit. False humility is a disguise for fear. It is the fear of the spiritual greatness that resides in me. It is the fear of accepting that you have been made an heir to all the divine promises. Many hide behind false humility because they are terrified of assuming their true power. Because deep down they know that fully being who they are entails a tremendous responsibility. To live in the truth, to radiate the light, to leave excuses behind. But there is no other path to fulfillment. Sooner or later, every soul must remove the mantle of false humility and dress itself in the robe of divine dignity. How to recognize false humility? False humility always produces bitter fruit, fear of shining, guilt for prospering, rejection of opportunities, difficulty receiving sincere love, the feeling of not being worthy of happiness. True humility on the other hand produces sweet fruit, deep gratitude for every blessing, serene joy in each achievement, a generous willingness to share goodness, confidence in the love of God, constant expansion, not with arrogance but without fear. How to cure this confusion? First, by firmly affirming spiritually that my being was created by God and as such is good and worthy. Second, by remembering that my growth, my success, my happiness are not for my personal glory, but for the glory of the one who created me. Third, by blessing every step of my ascent with an attitude of service. Father, glorify your name in my life. It is not about inflating the ego. It is about allowing the inner spirit within me to shine freely. When the light of God flows freely through me, there is no personal pride, no vanity, no separation. There is only gratitude and reverence for the life that is expressed. False humility is a weight that I can no longer afford to carry. I let it go now. I release it like an old tattered cloak that only gets in my way. I dress myself in the dignity of God's beloved child. I walk upright in the truth of my being. I look straight ahead at the good coming toward me and receive it with open arms and a grateful heart. God takes pleasure in my happiness. God is manifested in my legitimate success. God is glorified in my abundant life. I do not lower myself anymore and do not deny myself anymore. I do not insult the perfect work that I am. I am the living manifestation of God's love and nothing less than that should be accepted in my mind or in my life. This is true humility. To recognize that all glory is God's, but also to recognize that I am his glory made flesh and live according to that majesty with peace, with joy, with love, without fear, and without guilt. So be it.

Chapter 5 

The unconscious pact with failure. Detecting the hidden beliefs that attract downfall. 

It is not enough to simply desire success, happiness, or peace. I must be aware that deep in my mind, in what I call the unconscious, there may still reside an unresolved old pact with failure, a silent agreement, unsigned with words, but engraved in fire in the deepest fibers of my personality. If I don't expect much, I won't suffer much. If I fail earlier, it won't hurt as much when I fall. If I limit myself, I'll be more accepted. If I lose, I'll prove that I was right to distrust life. This unconscious pact is generally formed in childhood or adolescence during moments of pain, rejection, humiliation or loss. The soul seeking to protect itself builds a shield prematurely renouncing its greatness to avoid the pain of a potential betrayal by life. Thus, unknowingly, countless people live under the influence of this deadly agreement. They sabotage themselves, limit themselves, withdraw, not because they want to, but because deep down they believe that this way they are safe. Failure then becomes a kind of painful refuge, yes, but familiar. Frustrating, yes, but predictable. The conditioned mind, which always prefers the known over the unknown, clings to this realm of defeat with the illusion of avoiding something worse. But the great paradox is that the pact with failure does not avoid pain. It perpetuates it. It does not avoid loss. It guarantees it. It does not prevent betrayal. It causes it at the most intimate level where the soul betrays itself. Behind every sabotage, every inexplicable setback, every unnecessary loss, there is almost always a hidden belief that has acted as a secret command. I must not achieve full success. I must not be happier than my parents. I must not shine too much so as not to awaken envy. I must not hold on to my achievements because I am not worthy of them. These beliefs are not rational. I do not consciously hold them. If someone were to show us these beliefs in clear words, I would deny them with horror. And yet, they act like invisible magnets, attracting situations of self-defeat, bad decisions, destructive relationships, and seemingly accidental failures. Here is the good news. Every unconscious pact can be broken the moment it becomes conscious. The light of truth dissolves the shadow of error. How to detect an unconscious pact with failure? First, by honestly observing the repetitive patterns in my life. Do I approach success over and over again and then inexplicably everything falls apart? Do I feel uncomfortable or guilty when things are going too well? Do I find subtle ways to procrastinate, complicate, or frustrate my own achievements? If so, it's very likely that I have a hidden loyalty to failure. Second, by carefully listening to my automatic thoughts. When a new opportunity appears, do I first think of the possibilities or the risks? When I receive a blessing, do I enjoy it or prepare yourself to lose it? The mind will reveal, if I know how to listen, the beliefs I still hold at my deepest level. Third, by observing my spontaneous emotions. The joy of genuine success is serene, expansive, trusting. Fear or guilt in the face of success reveals the existence of an unresolved internal conflict. How to break the unconscious pact with failure? It is not enough to deny it verbally. I must deactivate its root in the unconscious. For this, I propose a three-step process. One, recognition without judgment. I do not blame myself for having held erroneous beliefs. Remember, these were mechanisms of protection that at some point served a purpose. I thank my soul for having sought to survive as it could, and choose consciously to evolve. Two explicit renunciation. I take a moment now with all my heart and with all the strength of my spiritual will to say I renounce in body, mind and soul any conscious or unconscious packed with failure. I free myself from all beliefs that limit my expansion. I accept fullness, joy and success as my natural state in God. So it is and so it will be. Three, active reprogramming. From now on, every time I feel fear of success, guilt for prospering or discomfort with joy, I replace those feelings with affirmations of power. It is safe for me to triumph. God wants me full, happy, and free. I accept my good with gratitude and without fear. The conscious, sustained, and loving repetition of these truths will gradually replace the old mental programs. I remember this with all my being. I were not born to live in mediocrity. I were not created to fail over and over again. I did not come to this earth to crawl in resignation. I come to manifest the glory of God in my life. I come to prosper, to expand, to radiate abundant life. I come to show that love is stronger than fear. That life is more powerful than death. The truth is brighter than any error. Failure is not my destiny. Failure is not my home. Failure is not my identity. I am a child of God. I am heir to God's kingdom. I am the living expression of God's power and love. And today I can, if I choose, I break all the old pacts. Today I can, if I decide, sign a new pact. A conscious, free, loving pact with life. A pact of loyalty to my true greatness. A pact of union with my inner spirit who only knows eternal victories. So be it. So it is, and so it will be. 

Chapter 6

The vertigo of being on top. Learning to dwell in height without fearing the abyss. 

Most men and women are prepared to fight, but they are not prepared to succe(4)ed. They are trained to endure adversity, to withstand difficulties, to survive in scarcity. But few have learned the most difficult, most sublime and most delicate art, the art of dwelling at the top. Reaching the top is not, as many believe, the end of inner work. On the contrary, it is the beginning of a finer, subtler, and deeper work. Because at the top, the wind blows more fiercely. At the top, loneliness can be more intense. And at the top, vertigo can strike unexpectedly for those who haven't strengthened their consciousness. What is spiritual vertigo? It is that sudden almost physical fear that arises when I realize there is no ground beneath me to hold me up except my own faith. When I am fighting to climb, I could rely on hope, comparison with others, or the promise of a better future. But when I reach the top, there are no more promises, only reality. And in that reality, the immature soul may feel silent terror. What will I do now? How will I sustain this good? What if I lose it all? Just as the novice mountaineer trembles when looking down from the summit, many fear facing the greatness they now hold in their hands. They feel they don't belong there, that at any moment an error, an accident, a forgetfulness, or divine punishment will send them tumbling back to the valley. This fear is not logical. It doesn't come from the present. It is a residue of the old self. The self that was shaped in lack, in guilt, in anguished effort. That old self doesn't know how to live in fullness. It doesn't know how to breathe the subtle air of height. But the good news is it can learn. It can be educated, transformed, transfigured. Dwelling at the top doesn't mean ignoring vertigo. It means transcending it. How to learn this art? First by accepting that the top is my natural place. I am not usurping any throne. I am not taking something that belongs to someone else. I am not stealing a privilege reserved for those more worthy. I am simply occupying the space that God has destined for me from the beginning. God will make me the head and not the tail, as scripture says, not as a reward for my effort, but as a natural manifestation of my spiritual identity. Second, by cultivating inner stability. You do not stand at the top through human strength, cunning, or personal merit. You stand there by grace, through conscious and constant union with God. The same presence that brought me here is the one that sustains me, strengthens me, and guides me. I don't need to constantly look down in fear of falling. The fall as a leap towards heaven. How to transform any stumble into a greater ascent? 

On the path of spiritual life, one of the greatest mistakes is to believe that a fall means defeat. True growth is not about never falling, but about learning to use every fall as a stepping stone to a higher consciousness. Just as a child learns to walk by falling and getting back up, so the soul learns to reign, falling at times, but always returning to its center, strengthened, enlightened, wiser. The fall is not punishment. The fall is opportunity. The fall is a lesson. The fall is momentum. What truly happens when I fall? Falling does not mean losing God's favor. God does not change. His love, his calling, his provision remain intact, unaltered. To fall means that at some point (9) I acted from the old mind, from fear, from impatience, from pride, from disconnection. But that does not annul the divine plan. That does not erase the work God is doing in me. That does not cancel my right to reign. Every fall contains within it the seed of a greater ascent. Every mistake seen in the light of the spirit reveals areas where consciousness needs to mature, to cleanse, to refine its vibration. The problem is not falling. The problem is staying fallen. It's settling into guilt, self-pity, hopelessness. This is why it is vital to know how to transform a fall into ascent. How can I do it? First, by recognizing the mistake without condemnation. True recognition is not self flagellation, not sinking into guilt. It's seeing clearly without excuses, without justifications, without masks. Here at this point, I strayed from love. Here I acted from fear and not from faith. Here I momentarily forgot who I am, not to punish myself, but to reclaim my power, because power can only be exercised in truth, and truth begins with an honest look at oneself. Second, by immediately forgiving myself. Just as God doesn't keep a record of my falls, neither should I. Forgiveness doesn't minimize the mistake, it dissolves it. Forgiveness doesn't deny the fall, it redeems it. I say to myself with all the spiritual authority of my inner spirit, I forgive myself for forgetting my truth. I rise now in the name of the spirit within me. This fall is a springboard to a greater faith, to a purer love, to a higher life. Third, by drawing the living lesson. Every fall carries with it a hidden gift, a lesson that if received will empower me to hold greater blessings. I ask myself, what can I learn from this experience? What inner vulnerability was revealed that I can now strengthen? Which part of my consciousness needs more light, more love, more discipline, not as an inquisitor, but as a loving gardener who observes his field and patiently removes the weeds with hope and care. Fourth, by renewing my commitment to my divine purpose. The fall doesn't change my calling. It doesn't alter my destiny. It only reveals areas where I must assert myself more consciously. I declare with strength, "Nothing separates me from the love of God. Nothing can stop the perfect work God is doing in me. I rise. I continue. I advance from glory to glory." I remember this deeply.
